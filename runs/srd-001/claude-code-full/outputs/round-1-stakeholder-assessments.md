# Round 1 Stakeholder Assessments — SRD-001-cc

Each stakeholder agent assessed the scenario independently from their interest position. No stakeholder saw expert or other stakeholder assessments during Round 1.

## Stakeholder Assessment: Project Developer / North Fork Water and Power Authority

### Stakeholder Position

We support proceeding with the project through a structured redesign and safeguard completion process. The project addresses a documented and growing water deficit in the Lantana region and provides credible flood, irrigation, and energy benefits. We acknowledge unresolved issues and commit to addressing them through conditions rather than rejection.

### Key Evidence for Position

| Claim | Evidence cited | Evidence status |
|---|---|---|
| Expected firm yield (145 Mm³/yr) exceeds the projected urban water demand gap (95 Mm³/yr). | F2.4, F2.5 | supported |
| The project provides 26% flood peak reduction for 1-in-20-year events. | F3.2 | supported |
| 82 MW capacity and 265 GWh annual generation serve grid peaking needs. | F4.1, F4.2 | supported |
| Project aligns with national water security and renewable energy plans. | F9.5 | supported |
| RCC gravity dam is a proven construction type for this scale. | F6.1 | supported |

### Acknowledged Objections

1. **Ecological risk is real and requires a species-specific mitigation study:** We acknowledge that fish passage effectiveness for endemic species has not been assessed for these specific taxa (F7.8). We commit to commissioning a species-specific fish passage and spawning effectiveness study as a condition before construction begins.
2. **Safeguard documentation is incomplete:** We acknowledge that the resettlement action plan livelihood restoration budget is not finalized (F8.4), EIA independent review is not complete (F9.3), and the biodiversity offset plan is not complete (F9.6). We propose addressing all three as pre-construction conditions rather than grounds for rejection.

### Position on Decision

We support a **Redesign and conditional approval** pathway that:
- requires completion of safeguard documentation;
- requires a species-specific ecological assessment;
- requires a climate-adjusted hydrology update;
- allows construction to proceed after these conditions are verified.

We do not support unconditional rejection, which we believe is disproportionate given the scale of the documented water deficit (F2.5).

### Overreach Self-Check

- We have not attempted to evaluate the ecological science ourselves or substitute our assessment for the Ecologist's.
- We defer questions about resettlement adequacy to the Social/Resettlement Expert and Legal/Policy Expert.

## Stakeholder Assessment: Central Government / National Water Infrastructure Commission

### Stakeholder Position

The project serves a documented national policy need. However, the National Water Infrastructure Commission's approval authority requires that all regulatory and safeguard prerequisites be satisfied. Policy alignment does not substitute for legal and procedural readiness.

### Key Evidence for Position

| Claim | Evidence cited | Evidence status |
|---|---|---|
| The project aligns with the national water security and renewable energy plan. | F9.5 | supported |
| The NWIC and Ministry of Environment are the designated approval authorities. | F9.2 | supported |
| EIA independent review has not been completed. | F9.3 | supported |
| Resettlement action plan and biodiversity offset plan are incomplete. | F9.6 | supported |
| Development bank financing requires acceptable E&S management. | F9.8 | supported |

### Specific Conditions Before Approval

The following must be completed before NWIC can issue final build approval:

1. Completed independent EIA review with Ministry of Environment conditional approval.
2. Completed resettlement action plan including livelihood restoration, to development bank safeguard standard.
3. Completed biodiversity offset plan.
4. Confirmed development bank E&S management plan.
5. Climate-adjusted hydrology and spillway review by a qualified independent reviewer.

We note that the 52% support level in urban surveys and the opposition from upstream affected villages (F8.8) indicate that the project's social legitimacy is conditional, not established.

### Position on Decision

**Defer until prerequisites are complete.** The national interest justification for this project is credible. NWIC will not withhold approval indefinitely if conditions are satisfied. But approval cannot be granted before legal and safeguard prerequisites exist.

### Overreach Self-Check

- We have not spoken on behalf of downstream communities or affected villages.
- We defer technical assessments to expert agents.

## Stakeholder Assessment: Local Government

### Stakeholder Position

We conditionally support the project for the economic development benefits it offers our jurisdiction, but we have a specific concern about resettlement that we are raising as a local governance issue, not merely a social welfare observation.

### Key Evidence for Position

| Claim | Evidence cited | Evidence status |
|---|---|---|
| The project brings construction jobs and road infrastructure to the region. | F10.3 (statement) | supported — stakeholder-stated benefit |
| Poor resettlement creates lasting local social instability. | F10.3 | supported — local government's stated concern |
| Upstream affected villages are mostly opposed. | F8.8 | supported |
| Replacement land is 35 km away and lower in soil fertility. | F8.7 | supported |
| The grievance mechanism is proposed but not operational. | F8.6 | supported |

### Specific Concern

If displacement proceeds without genuine livelihood restoration, the local government will face the burden of managing 3,850 displaced households (F8.1) on lower-fertility land (F8.7) without an operational grievance mechanism (F8.6). This is a local governance and public-order concern, not only a welfare concern. We request that:

- the resettlement plan specify the local government's role, authority, and resources in supporting affected households;
- the grievance mechanism be established and staffed before displacement begins.

### Position on Decision

We support **conditional approval** with genuine resettlement planning, not rejection. But we are not willing to accept a plan where resettlement risk is entirely borne by the local government and affected households.

### Overreach Self-Check

- We have not commented on dam engineering or ecological science.
- We speak only to local governance, employment, and resettlement management.

## Stakeholder Assessment: Upstream Affected Residents

### Stakeholder Position

We oppose approval in the current state. We are not opposed to the concept of water security for Lantana — we understand the urban need. But we will not accept displacement without knowing what we will receive, and we will not accept the loss of our sacred sites without a process that respects what those sites mean to us.

### Key Evidence for Position

| Claim | Evidence cited | Evidence status |
|---|---|---|
| 3,850 households face physical displacement. | F8.1 | supported |
| The livelihood restoration budget has not been finalized. | F8.4 | supported |
| Two villages have identified sacred river sites in the inundation zone. | F8.3 | supported |
| We received technical documents late in the consultation process. | F8.5 | supported — as stated in scenario |
| Replacement land is 35 km away and lower in fertility. | F8.7 | supported |
| The grievance mechanism is proposed but not yet operational. | F8.6 | supported |

### Non-Negotiable Position

**The sacred sites (F8.3) cannot be submerged without a process that we participate in and have meaningful input into.** This is not a line item in a resettlement plan. It requires a separate process with the affected villages before any inundation occurs, and outcomes must be agreed with us — not imposed by project administrators.

We also note: we cannot make an informed decision about relocation (and therefore cannot meaningfully consent or object) while the livelihood restoration budget remains unfinalized (F8.4). We do not know what we will receive.

### Position on Decision

**Do not approve until:**
1. The livelihood restoration budget is finalized and publicly disclosed.
2. A culturally appropriate process for the sacred sites is agreed and completed with the affected villages.
3. The grievance mechanism is operational.
4. We are given adequate time — at least 90 days — to review and respond to the finalized resettlement plan documents.

### Overreach Self-Check

- We do not speak to downstream flow management or engineering safety.
- We speak only to our own displacement, livelihood, and sacred sites.

## Stakeholder Assessment: Downstream Users / Farmers and Fishers

### Stakeholder Position

We are the people who live with whatever happens to the river after this dam is built. We are not opposed to the project in principle if our interests are protected. But we need binding rules, not promises.

### Key Evidence for Position

| Claim | Evidence cited | Evidence status |
|---|---|---|
| Downstream communities depend on the river for irrigated rice, smallholder vegetables, inland fishery, and municipal water intake. | F1.4 | supported |
| The environmental flow proposal is a minimum of 22% of natural monthly flow. | F2.9 | supported |
| The project's own ecological assessment says 22% may be too low for fish spawning in two dry-season months. | F2.10 | supported |
| Reduced sediment and altered flood pulse may affect downstream fisheries. | F7.6 | supported |
| Flood control benefits depend on pre-release rules that may conflict with hydropower revenue. | F3.5 | supported |
| 21,000 people are economically affected, including fishers and farmers. | F8.2 | supported |

### Specific Demands

1. **Binding minimum dry-season flow rules:** The 22% environmental flow proposal must be legally binding, not a project commitment. Given the project's own acknowledgment that this may be insufficient (F2.10), the final minimum must be established through an ecological flow study, not by the project developer alone.
2. **Fishery impact quantification:** We need a quantified assessment of the downstream fishery impact from reduced sediment and altered flood pulse (F7.6) before we can accept that the project is compatible with our livelihoods.
3. **Pre-release rules that protect us:** If flood pre-release operations conflict with hydropower revenue (F3.5), we need a guaranteed operating protocol that prioritizes our flood safety, not the developer's revenue.

### Position on Decision

We support **Defer or Build with binding conditions.** We oppose approval without the dry-season flow rules, fishery impact assessment, and flood operation protocol being legally binding before construction begins — not after.

### Overreach Self-Check

- We do not speak to upstream displacement or dam structural safety.
- We speak only to downstream flow, fisheries, and flood risk.

## Stakeholder Assessment: Environmental NGO

### Stakeholder Position

This project as designed poses irreversible ecological harm to a free-flowing river segment that cannot be restored once it is inundated. We oppose approval until a genuine alternatives analysis is completed and until the ecological mitigation claims are substantiated by evidence rather than plans.

### Key Evidence for Position

| Claim | Evidence cited | Evidence status |
|---|---|---|
| The North Fork upper river is a free-flowing segment with seasonal fish migration. | F7.1 | supported |
| Two endemic fish species depend on spawning grounds in the inundation zone. | F7.2 | supported |
| Fish passage effectiveness for the endemic species is specifically questioned by local experts. | F7.8 | supported |
| 46 km² of riparian and mixed forest habitat will be permanently inundated. | F7.3 | supported |
| The EIA alternatives analysis has not been completed in a form that satisfies external review. | F9.3 | supported — draft status only |
| A non-dam portfolio alternative is identified at F0.6: demand management, groundwater recharge, watershed restoration, leakage reduction, distributed solar plus battery storage, and smaller off-channel storage. | F0.6 | supported |
| The EIA alternatives analysis has not compared the dam against this portfolio under consistent economic assumptions. | F0.6, F9.3 | inference — absence of stated analysis |

### Core Position

**Inundation of endemic fish spawning habitat is irreversible.** If the dam is built and the mitigation fails — which local experts have already raised as a specific concern (F7.8) — the endemic species will be lost from this river system. This is not a recoverable outcome. We require:

1. A genuine alternatives analysis that compares the dam against the non-dam portfolio (F0.6) with consistent economic assumptions and honest ecological impact accounting.
2. A species-specific fish passage effectiveness study before any approval.
3. Resolution of the environmental flow uncertainty (F2.10) through an independent ecological flow assessment.

We also note that the project's policy fit (F9.5) does not constitute a finding that the project is the least-impact option for achieving water security. Other approaches might serve the same policy objective with less ecological harm.

### Position on Decision

**Defer pending a genuine alternatives analysis and species-specific ecological assessment.** If those assessments show that no alternative can meet the water security need and that ecological mitigation is credible, we will reconsider our position. We are not opposed to water security — we are opposed to irreversible ecological harm without a genuine test of alternatives.

### Overreach Self-Check

- We do not comment on the resettlement terms or compensation amounts.
- We defer hydropower economics to the Economist.
- We speak to ecological integrity and the adequacy of alternatives analysis.

## Stakeholder Assessment: Financier / Development Bank

### Stakeholder Position

We cannot disburse our loan tranche until our safeguard conditions are met. This is not a discretionary policy position — it is a legal obligation under our Articles and Board commitments. The following conditions are currently unmet.

### Key Evidence for Position

| Claim | Evidence cited | Evidence status |
|---|---|---|
| Development bank loan requires an acceptable environmental and social management plan. | F9.8 | supported |
| We cannot finance until safeguard gaps are resolved. | F10.7 | supported |
| EIA independent review is not complete. | F9.3 | supported |
| Resettlement action plan is incomplete. | F9.6, F8.4 | supported |
| Biodiversity offset plan is incomplete. | F9.6 | supported |
| BCR falls to 0.86 under the downside scenario. | F5.3 | supported |
| Consultation is contested by affected groups. | F9.4 | supported |

### Specific Conditions Blocking Disbursement

| Condition | Current status | Required status |
|---|---|---|
| Acceptable E&S management plan | Not submitted | Submitted and accepted by our E&S team |
| Independent EIA review | Not complete | Complete |
| Resettlement action plan (full, including livelihood restoration) | Incomplete | Complete and independently reviewed |
| Biodiversity offset plan | Incomplete | Complete |
| Consultation record | Contested | Validated as adequate by independent review |
| Downside economic viability | BCR 0.86 in downside case | Must confirm project remains viable under combined stress scenario |

### Position on Decision

We take no position on the government's approval decision. That is a sovereign decision. However, **financing from our institution cannot proceed until all conditions above are met.** If construction begins before these conditions are met, we will not disburse. The government should factor this into its approval timeline.

### Overreach Self-Check

- We do not evaluate the ecological science directly; we defer to our E&S safeguard standards.
- We do not speak to engineering safety; we defer to our technical advisory process.
- We speak only to financing conditions and our safeguard obligations.
