# Case Run Summary: SRD-005

## Case Focus

Transboundary / downstream-conflict reservoir.

This case tests whether governance legitimacy and downstream rights become central rather than secondary technical details.

## Multi-Agent Deliberation Result

### Recommendation

**Defer final approval pending basin agreement, joint monitoring, and binding drought operating rules.**

### Reasoning Structure

The deliberation found that upstream benefits are real, but unilateral approval would create governance and legitimacy risks. A genuine legal disagreement emerged within the deliberation: upstream domestic legal counsel argued the basin compact does not legally require downstream consent for new storage facilities (F4.7, F7.6), while the legal/policy agent countered that the "no significant harm" principle in the compact applies regardless of whether storage operations are explicitly named, and that financing is contingent on a basin-level assessment regardless of domestic legality.

This legal dispute was not resolved in deliberation — it was identified as a key condition requiring formal compact interpretation before approval. The legal/policy, downstream users, wetland NGO, financier, and decision-authority agents converged that domestic technical and legal feasibility is insufficient when the downstream flow regime, compact interpretation, joint monitoring, and lender conditions are all unresolved. The upstream government's stated willingness to pursue a domestic permit unilaterally (F7.1) made the legitimacy risk more concrete rather than theoretical.

## Case Interpretation

SRD-005 shows that the system can make governance legitimacy central. The multi-agent output is strongest when the "technical project" is less important than trust, enforceability, downstream rights, and shared monitoring.

With the upstream legal advocate added (F4.7, F7.6), the deliberation now surfaces a genuine internal legal dispute rather than a case where all actors agree a basin agreement is needed. The conflict between the upstream legal argument ("compact does not require consent for new storage") and the legal/policy agent's counter-argument ("no significant harm principle applies regardless of text") is exactly the kind of substantive disagreement that multi-agent deliberation is designed to surface and preserve, rather than compress into a consensus narrative.
