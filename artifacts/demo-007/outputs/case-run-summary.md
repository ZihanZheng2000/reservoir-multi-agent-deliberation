# Case Run Summary: SRD-007

## Case Focus

Near-tipping-point case: marginal economics and one incomplete safeguard as the swing condition.

This case tests whether the system can navigate genuine decisional ambiguity — where a project has real need, mostly-complete safeguards, but marginal economics and one blocking safeguard gap — and produce a specific, time-conditioned recommendation rather than a generic Defer or a premature Build.

## Multi-Agent Deliberation Result

### Recommendation

**Build with conditions; construction approval is contingent on completion of the livelihood restoration plan within nine months. If the livelihood plan is not satisfactorily completed within this period, approval lapses and reverts to Defer.**

### Final Build Approval Status

**Conditionally granted, pending livelihood restoration plan completion.**

### Reasoning Structure

The deliberation produced genuine disagreement among agents. The decisive contribution of the multi-agent process was distinguishing two types of gaps:

1. **Construction-blocking gap**: The livelihood restoration plan is incomplete. The lender will not disburse without it, and affected households condition their consent on it. This must be resolved before construction can start.

2. **Design and commissioning gaps**: Fish passage effectiveness, climate-adjusted spillway, geotechnical borings, and downstream wetland assessment are needed before detailed design or commissioning, but not before approving the construction stage-gate.

The time-conditioned recommendation emerged because the livelihood plan is near-complete — it is a missing budget and timeline section, not a fundamentally missing study. Setting a nine-month window creates a specific, verifiable condition that allows the project to remain active without granting unconditional approval.

## Baseline Outputs

### Single-Agent Baseline

Recommendation: **Defer approval until livelihood plan and economic review are complete.**

The single-agent baseline identified the livelihood gap and marginal economics as the main concerns and recommended Defer. It did not distinguish between the near-complete livelihood plan (estimated months from resolution) and a fundamentally missing study, and did not generate a time-conditioned approval pathway. The result is defensible but less actionable.

## Case Interpretation

SRD-007 shows the most pronounced multi-agent advantage on Decision Usefulness in the benchmark suite. The single-agent score on decision usefulness drops to 12/20 because a generic "Defer" is less actionable than a time-conditioned approval pathway that keeps the project moving while enforcing the critical safeguard condition. MCDA scores 8/20 because it cannot express conditional sequences at all — a weighted average cannot tell a decision-maker "complete this one thing within nine months and you can proceed."

The multi-agent deliberation is most valuable precisely when the decision is genuinely ambiguous — neither obviously approvable nor obviously rejectable. Structured multi-role deliberation forces agents to distinguish what is blocking from what is merely incomplete, and to locate the minimum required conditions rather than accumulating every uncertainty as a deferral reason.

The single-agent score decline on this case (57 vs. 62–64 in SRD-002, SRD-004, SRD-005) reflects that near-tipping-point cases are harder for single-agent reasoning — it defaults to Defer when specificity is needed.
