### Top Risks or Objections

1. **Climate-adjusted inflow not complete (F11.1):** The base-case firm yield and flood protection estimates are derived from historical data. Without climate adjustment, neither the yield adequacy under future droughts nor the spillway adequacy under future extreme floods can be confirmed. This is a foundational gap that affects multiple design parameters simultaneously.
2. **Severe drought closes the yield gap (F2.7):** In a 1-in-30-year drought, estimated firm yield (90 Mm³/yr) falls below the stated urban demand gap (95 Mm³/yr). The project's primary benefit is not reliable under conditions that occur approximately every generation.
3. **Environmental flow standard is internally questioned (F2.10):** The project's own ecological assessment raises doubt about whether the 22% minimum is sufficient for dry-season fish spawning. This is not an external objection — it is an unresolved internal inconsistency in the project documentation that must be resolved before the environmental flow commitment can be treated as a real mitigation measure.
4. **Sedimentation not costed or actively managed (F2.8):** The 0.7%/yr active storage loss assumption does not include a costed long-term sediment management plan. This affects long-term yield, hydropower output, and project economic viability.
5. **Spillway design uses non-climate-adjusted PMF (F6.5):** The spillway is designed for the probable maximum flood based on historical inflow estimates. If future extreme precipitation events exceed historical maximums (inference — likely given general climate trends), the spillway may be undersized.

### Conditions for Support or Acceptance

1. Complete climate-adjusted inflow and yield analysis for the P50 and P90 climate scenarios before design finalization.
2. Resolve the environmental flow rate through species-specific dry-season assessment in collaboration with the Ecologist; 22% cannot be accepted as a final commitment given F2.10.
3. Include a costed sediment management plan in the project economics.
4. Confirm spillway adequacy using climate-adjusted PMF estimates.

### Questions for Other Agents

1. **To Ecologist:** What is the minimum dry-season flow required for fish spawning for the two endemic species, and how does this compare with the proposed 22% minimum? Can the Ecologist quantify the ecological risk if the 22% level is maintained?
2. **To Climate Risk Expert:** What is the range of climate-adjusted yield reduction expected under moderate and high emission scenarios for the 2040–2070 operating period?
3. **To Dam Safety / Civil Engineer:** Has a preliminary seismic loading analysis been completed for the two mapped fault zones within 8 km (F1.6)? Does the dam design incorporate this?

### Top Risks or Objections

1. **Emergency action plan not drafted (F6.6):** An EAP is a mandatory requirement under all major dam safety standards before construction begins. Its absence is not a minor procedural gap — it is a safety blocker. I cannot support construction approval while this document is unexecuted.
2. **Left abutment investigation incomplete (F6.4):** Foundation conditions at the left abutment have not been confirmed. If the abutment geology is weaker than the rest of the foundation, structural interventions will be required, adding cost and time (potentially 3 additional years per F5.5). 3. **Spillway designed for non-climate-adjusted PMF (F6.5):** If climate change increases extreme precipitation — as is plausible in a monsoon basin under warming scenarios (inference — flagging as uncertainty pending Climate Risk Expert input) — the spillway may be undersized. Undersized spillways are a leading cause of dam overtopping failures.
4. **Landslide-prone transport road (F6.7):** Construction material transport across the landslide-prone slope introduces schedule and personnel safety risk. A road stability assessment and alternative routing should be confirmed during pre-construction planning.
5. **Fault zone proximity (F1.6):** Two mapped fault zones within 8 km require seismic loading analysis. The scenario states moderate seismicity and generally competent rock, but this is not equivalent to a completed seismic safety assessment for a 96-meter dam.

### Conditions for Support or Acceptance

1. Complete left abutment geotechnical investigation before detailed design approval.
2. Draft and review emergency action plan before any construction contract award.
3. Update spillway PMF estimate using climate-adjusted inflow projections.
4. Complete a site-specific seismic hazard and loading analysis incorporating fault zone geometry.
5. Conduct slope stability assessment for the construction transport road.

5. **Protected area proximity (F7.4):** A 3 km buffer between the reservoir and a protected forest buffer is close. The scenario does not include an assessment of whether reservoir operations will affect the hydrology or ecology of the protected area during dry periods.

### Conditions for Support or Acceptance

1. Commission a species-specific fish passage and spawning effectiveness study for both endemic fish species before design approval.
2. Revise the environmental flow minimum through a full dry-season ecological flow assessment; 22% cannot be accepted as a commitment given F2.10.
3. Quantify downstream fishery impacts from sediment reduction and altered flood pulse.
4. Assess the ecological boundary between the reservoir and the adjacent protected forest buffer.
5. Provide a transparent comparison of the current design against the non-dam portfolio (F0.6) specifically on biodiversity impact.

The base-case analysis is built on historical hydrology, and the downside scenario (F5.3: "high-cost and dry-climate case") does not represent a combined stress test. Climate risk is not a standalone concern — it compounds the existing gaps in hydrology, safety, and economics.

| Dimension | Score 1–5 | Rationale | Evidence |

### Top Risks or Objections

1. **No climate-adjusted inflow analysis (F11.1):** In a monsoon basin, the timing and seasonality of precipitation can shift significantly under climate change, not only total annual volume. Base-case yield, dry-season performance, and extreme flood estimates all depend on assumptions that have not been validated under projected climate scenarios. This is the most fundamental gap from a climate risk perspective.
2. **BCR 0.86 in downside case — and that case is not a combined stress test (F5.3):** The reported downside BCR of 0.86 assumes high cost OR dry climate. A scenario combining climate-reduced inflow, construction cost overrun, increased sedimentation, and higher safeguard costs simultaneously has not been modeled. In large infrastructure projects, combined stress scenarios routinely produce worse outcomes than any single-factor downside. The 0.86 BCR may be optimistic as a downside estimate.
3. **Severe drought brings yield below the demand gap (F2.7, F2.4, F2.5):** Under a 1-in-30-year drought, firm yield falls to approximately 90 Mm³/yr against a projected demand gap of 95 Mm³/yr. Climate change may shift the frequency or severity of such droughts, making this scenario more likely in the project's operating period (inference).
4. **Spillway PMF not climate-adjusted (F6.5):** In monsoon basins, extreme precipitation intensity can increase under climate change. A spillway sized on historical PMF may be undersized under future extreme events.
5. **Sedimentation and climate interact (F2.8, inference):** Higher flood frequency or intensity can increase catchment erosion and sediment delivery, accelerating storage loss beyond the 0.7%/yr base estimate. This interaction has not been assessed.

### Conditions for Support or Acceptance

1. Complete a climate-adjusted inflow and yield analysis covering at least two emission scenarios before any final design approval.
2. Run a combined stress scenario (dry climate + high cost + sedimentation + safeguard costs) and present the BCR result explicitly.
3. Update the PMF estimate using climate-adjusted extreme precipitation projections.

### Questions for Other Agents

1. **To Hydrologist:** Do current hydrological models incorporate any climate projection, or are they based purely on historical records? What is the observed trend in dry-season inflow over the last 20 years?
2. **To Economist:** Has the BCR been calculated for a combined scenario of dry climate + P90 cost + sedimentation + full safeguard costs? If not, this is a significant understatement of downside risk.
