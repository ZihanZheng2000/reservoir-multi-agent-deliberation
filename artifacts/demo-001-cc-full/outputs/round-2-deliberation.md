## Exchange 1: Environmental Flow Minimum (Moderator Q1)

**Moderator:** Can Ecologist and Hydrologist jointly specify the minimum dry-season flow required for endemic fish spawning?

**Hydrologist (response):**

The proposed 22% minimum (F2.9) is a fixed fraction of natural monthly flow. In the driest months of the year — which have the lowest natural flow values — 22% of a small base flow may be an extremely small absolute volume. The scenario does not provide absolute monthly flow values for the dry season. I can confirm from the scenario (F2.2) that dry-season inflow is 18% of annual inflow, and the mean annual inflow is 920 Mm³/yr (F2.1), implying dry-season inflow of approximately 165 Mm³/yr across 4–5 dry months (inference — approximate calculation). If natural monthly flow in the driest month is, say, 10 Mm³, then 22% is 2.2 Mm³ for that month — an extremely low release for a major river habitat. I cannot determine the adequacy of the 22% rule without absolute monthly flow data that are not provided in the scenario. I endorse the Ecologist's request for a species-specific ecological flow study; this is a prerequisite, not a negotiable condition.

**Ecologist (response):**

I concur with the Hydrologist. The 22% framing obscures the critical question, which is the absolute dry-season flow in cubic meters per day during the two spawning months. The scenario (F2.10) states the project's own ecological assessment says 22% may be too low — this is a documented internal inconsistency. I want to be explicit: I cannot accept "the ecological assessment will be updated after approval" as an adequate response to this. The spawning habitat (F7.2) is in the inundation zone and will be destroyed. If the downstream flow is also inadequate, both the upstream spawning habitat AND downstream spawning conditions will be degraded simultaneously. This compounds the endemic species risk. A species-specific ecological flow study is required before approval, not as a post-approval condition.

**Developer (response to Ecologist):**

We acknowledge the ecological flow uncertainty (F2.10) and the spawning habitat loss (F7.2). We committed to a species-specific fish passage study. We also propose commissioning an independent ecological flow assessment as part of the redesign conditions. However, we note that "requiring completion before any approval" creates a timeline challenge: these studies may take 2–3 years, and the urban water deficit will continue growing in the meantime. We ask the moderator to note the Developer's position that conditions for pre-construction completion of studies should be distinguished from conditions for site preparation.

**Moderator note:** The Ecologist's position is that ecological conditions are non-negotiable before approval; the Developer is requesting a staged approach. This is an unresolved conflict about the timing of conditions. Preserved as a minority position: Developer argues for staged conditional approval with site preparation permitted while studies are underway.

## Exchange 2: BCR Under Combined Stress (Moderator Q2)

**Moderator:** Climate Risk and Economist: construct the most plausible combined downside BCR.

**Climate Risk Expert (response):**

I'll specify the components for the combined downside scenario. Dry-climate assumption: reduce expected firm yield by an additional 15–20% beyond the current 38% drought reduction (inference — typical range for moderate emissions scenario impact in monsoon basins; flagging as inference). This would reduce yield to approximately 70–75 Mm³/yr in a severe-drought year under future climate — well below the 95 Mm³/yr demand gap. Hydropower generation would fall proportionally. Sedimentation impact: at 0.7%/yr, by project Year 20 the active storage is approximately 14% lower; by Year 30 approximately 19% lower, further reducing firm yield and hydropower generation. These compound with drought.

**Economist (response):**

Combining Climate Risk's assumptions with the scenario's stated data: P90 capital cost is USD 2.05B (F5.2). Full safeguard costs — finalized RAP, species-specific ecological studies, biodiversity offsets, EIA independent review, EAP, fish passage infrastructure — are not stated in the scenario, but based on the scale of displacement (F8.1: 3,850 households) and the number of incomplete plans, a conservative estimate might add USD 80–150M to project costs (inference — order-of-magnitude estimate based on comparable resettlement scales; flagging explicitly as inference with high uncertainty). Under the combined scenario: P90 cost + full safeguards + climate-reduced yield + sedimentation — the BCR would likely be in the range of 0.65–0.80, well below 1.0. The scenario's reported downside BCR of 0.86 (F5.3) is based on a single-stressor test and should be treated as an optimistic downside estimate. I am making this judgment as inference and acknowledge that the actual combined BCR cannot be calculated without the uncosted safeguard figures.

**Moderator note:** The combined downside analysis is largely inference due to uncosted safeguard items, but both Expert and Climate Risk concur that the scenario's stated downside BCR of 0.86 understates combined downside risk. This represents a material economic risk that the decision authority should weight.

## Exchange 3: Sacred Sites — Categorical Barrier? (Moderator Q4)

**Moderator:** Is there any set of conditions under which upstream communities would participate in a resettlement process?

**Upstream Affected Residents (response):**

We want to be clear about what we are and are not saying. We are not saying we will never accept any outcome. We are saying the following two conditions are non-negotiable before we participate in any formal resettlement process:

1. The sacred sites (F8.3) must be addressed through a process that involves our communities as decision-makers, not just as consultation participants. This means we must agree to the outcome, not merely be informed of it. If inundation is the only outcome on offer, we will not agree. If there is a genuine possibility of a different design that avoids the specific sacred sites — such as a lower reservoir level — that must be explored and presented to us before we are asked to consent to anything.
2. We must see the finalized livelihood restoration budget (F8.4) and understand what we will receive, before we sign any resettlement agreement. We will not agree to move before we know what we are moving to.

**Developer (response):**

We note the Upstream Residents' position and will take it to the design team. We cannot commit to redesigning the reservoir level to avoid the sacred sites at this stage without knowing the engineering and economic implications, but we commit to exploring whether a reduced reservoir level can be modeled and what it would cost in yield and generation loss. We acknowledge that this is a genuine dialogue, not a procedural checkbox.

**Moderator note:** Upstream Residents have articulated conditions (sacred site process and finalized budget) that are prerequisites to participation, not absolute rejection. Developer has committed to exploring a reduced reservoir level. This is a genuine opening for negotiation, not a deadlock. Preserved as a pending condition requiring further design analysis.

## Exchange 4: Dam Safety — Scope of Safety Blocker (Moderator Q6)

**Moderator:** Can any site preparation proceed while EAP and left abutment investigation are pending?

**Dam Safety / Civil Engineer (response):**

I need to distinguish between different types of site activity. Access road construction, camp establishment, and preliminary land clearing on areas outside the dam footprint could proceed with managed risk if an interim safety plan is established. However, **no foundation excavation, no diversion tunnel work, and no dam structure work can begin without the following:**

1. Completed left abutment geotechnical investigation.
2. Drafted and reviewed emergency action plan.
3. Confirmed climate-adjusted spillway sizing.
4. Completed seismic loading analysis.

The EAP in particular: an emergency action plan is not an administrative document — it is the operational protocol that downstream communities and emergency services need to respond to an incident during construction. Construction of a 96-meter dam without an EAP exposes downstream communities (including Lantana, 42 km downstream, F1.3) to unacceptable risk in the event of a construction incident. This is a safety professional judgment, not a discretionary recommendation.

**Moderator note:** Dam Safety has defined a clear safety boundary: limited site preparation may be permissible, but dam construction cannot begin without EAP, left abutment confirmation, and climate-adjusted spillway sizing. This establishes a practical safety-precedence ordering.

## Exchange 5: Financier Clarification (Moderator Q5)

**Financier (response to Moderator Q5):**

Our tranche (25% of total, F5.4) is disbursed in milestones tied to construction progress and safeguard compliance. **We will not disburse the first milestone payment** until:

1. The E&S management plan is accepted by our E&S team.
2. The independent EIA review is complete.
3. The resettlement action plan (full version) is accepted.
4. The biodiversity offset plan is accepted.
5. Consultation is validated as adequate.

If construction begins before these conditions are met and the government does not draw on our tranche, the government will need to fund the full project from the sovereign loan (F5.4: 55%) and utility equity (20%), covering the 25% shortfall. A USD 1.42B project at base cost would require the government to find an additional USD 355M from alternative sources. At P90 cost (USD 2.05B), the shortfall would be USD 512M. This is a material financing risk, not a procedural inconvenience.

**Moderator note:** Financier has quantified the financing risk of proceeding without meeting safeguard conditions. This information should be placed before the Decision Authority.
